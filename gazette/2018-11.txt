== Teacher’s Corner – A Look at a Dance Devisor Much Admired by Teachers ==

by

'''Mel Whitson'''

'''T'''he repertoire of Scottish Country Dances has grown dramatically over the years.  A significant contributor to the collection was made by Roy Goldring, with 246 dances known dances, fourteen books of dances, and a variety of single dance leaflets.  His offerings are significant not just in sheer number but in quality.  Teachers often reach for books of his dances when putting together dance programs and lesson plans because his dances are reliably interesting and thoughtfully constructed.  Indeed, three out of the ten dances (Mary Erskine, The Ferry Louper, The Travelling Men) on the Portland Branch’s November 2018 dance program were written by Roy.

So who was Roy Goldring?  He was born in Devon, England and after serving in WWII he worked as a Civil Servant in a role that had him travelling around England.  When in Birmingham he met and later married his wife, Doreen.  When in Surrey he began to learn Scottish Country Dancing because he couldn’t find English Country Dancing in his locale.  He ultimately became a teacher and a member of the Leeds Branch and began to devise dances.  In 1977, one of his teachers at Summer School at St. Andrews submitted Roy’s dance, The Cuillins of Skye, to the RSCDS and it was published the following year.  He died on September 4, 2007.

When devising a dance, Roy sometimes sought to use existing figures that he thought should be more widely danced.  For example, his dance Argyll Strathspey was specifically written to highlight the Tournée.  He only created one original figure, the Capstan.  In general, he was a proponent of keeping dances simple and reportedly disliked dances with complex patterns, which he felt were lacking in dancing spirit and were more like geographic exercises.

His role as a teacher provided impetus for developing new dances.  He would create a dance in order to support his lesson plans to practice certain figures or steps.  Other dances were composed to help his class participants feel successful by dancing a relatively simple dance well.  He had an unusual ability to dream up dances that are easy but still stimulating for dancers.

In 2001, he was awarded the Scroll of Honor by the RSCDS and here is the citation that accompanied the presentation:  “Roy has been a member of the Leeds Branch since 1971, and has served two spells on the committee and four years as Chairman.  In addition, he has taught at all levels of Branch classes since obtaining his Teacher's Certificate in 1977.  Roy has also attended a variety of day and weekend schools.  However, Roy's claim to fame is as a deviser of a large number of dances, many of which have appeared in Society publications.  Many more have been published in leaflet form, with the proceeds often going to charities.  The main feature of Roy's dances is their accessibility to dancers of all abilities. Roy's dances have encouraged many to "join the dance", and his sensitivity to the music ensures their continuing popularity.”  Below is a picture of Roy being awarded his Scroll.

While teachers know many of his dances, dancers are more likely to recall having danced the following well known dances on dance programs:  The Reel of the Royal Scots, Argyll Strathspey, EH3 7AF, The Piper and the Penguin, Tribute to the Borders, The Minister on the Loch, Mary Erskine, Miss Johnstone of Ardrossan, The Music Makars, and St. Andrew’s Fair.  Interestingly, The Minister on the Loch was inspired by Henry Raeburn’s famous painting of The Skating Minister, pictured below.  Apparently Roy admired the painting and devised a dance with a strathspey poussette as he considered it to be the closest dance figure to skating.

== Scottish-American Wedding Traditions ==

by

'''Mel Whitson'''

'''C'''ontributed by Melissa Whitson and generally excerpted (with editorial liberties taken by the contributor) from the book The Plighted Troth, published by the RSCDS Orange County Branch

Scots were known for “nipping and scarting” during wooing, meaning that using a sharp tongue was the Scots way of showing love.  Here’s an example:  A gentle lover, who had observed all the proprieties with his sweetheart, said on becoming engaged, “Weel onywey I’ve been ceevil to ye.”  She replied, “Aye, senselessly ceevil, ye muckle gowk (big fool).”

The ceremony – brides’ attire and a few traditions:

 * Scottish wedding dresses were in pale pastel shades as well as white and in Scotland, velvet was sometimes favored for the fabric (gets cold in Scotland).
 * A veil was not worn but a wreath of white flowers was placed in the bride’s hair.
 * She would wear a plaid (tartan sash) pinned to her dress.  The groom may pin the sash to her shoulder during the ceremony, after they were pronounced “man and wife.”<br/>  Alternatively, the 
sash was presented to the bride by the groom or by the groom’s mother at the rehearsal dinner.
 * A sixpence would be placed in the bride’s shoe for good luck.
 * The bride and groom would link thumbs of their right hands and press them together as a vow of their fidelity.
 * An oatcake would be broken over the bride’s head to bring good luck, with all the unmarried ladies taking home a piece to put under their pillows for three nights, <br/>to (hopefully) result in dreams that revealed their future husband.

A selection of wedding celebration toasts:

 * Lang may your lum reek! (Long may our chimney smoke)
 * A cozy but, an’ a canty ben, ta couthie women and honest men! (A cozy kitchen, a cheerful parlor, to affectionate women and honest men)
 * May the moose ne’er leave your meal-pock wi’ the tear in its e’e! (May the mouse never leave your grain bag with a tear in its eye – in other words, may you always have grain)
 * The ingle neuk wi’ routh o’bannocks and bairns! (The chimney corner with abundance of oatcakes and children)
 * Here’s to a’ your fouk an’ a’ our fouk, an’ a’ the fouk that’s been kind to your fouk an’ if a’ fouk had aye been as kind to fouk as your fouk’s been to our fouk, <br/>there wad aye hae been guid fouk I’ the warld sin fouk ben fouks.
 * And here’s to a’ wha drink this night, and here’s to them what’s far awa’, and muckle joy and pur delight.
